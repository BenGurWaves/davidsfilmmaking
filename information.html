Bio

I am David Ivanyuck, a filmmaker and visual artist whose work straddles the line between experimental cinema and quiet narrative. My approach values texture and rhythm over tidy exposition; I aim to build frames that carry the weight of lived moments while still breathing like a piece of music. I treat the image as a place to keep memory and doubt in parallel — to let them sit in the same frame and watch what happens.

My earliest work began in the margins: late-night experiments with found footage, phone camera tests, and small edits that taught me how to listen to images. Without formal textbooks dictating procedure, I learned by breaking things. Those mistakes are visible in the films I make — grain, stray light, and half-heard audio that together feel more human than anything polished to perfection.

Collaboration has been my greatest teacher. Working with musicians, designers, and other filmmakers has sharpened my sense of timing and given me permission to be messy. I don’t make movies to impress; I make them to understand. Each piece is a conversation with a moment in time — a way to hold certain questions without pretending to have answers.

The work is often slow, sometimes brutal, and occasionally tender. It’s not a portfolio of hits; it’s a map of attempts. I keep making because the act of making changes how I recognize possibility. That hunger, more than praise or numbers, is what keeps me filming.

Contact

For collaborations, commissions, or conversation: I prefer focused, intentional contact. If you’d like to talk about a project — anything from a concept-stage music film to a longer experimental piece — email is the clearest place to start.

Email: dfilmmaking@gmail.com

I work best with people who bring clarity of purpose and a tolerance for risk. That could mean a small budget and big ambition, or a long timeline and an unknown result. I’m open to travel for projects that need it, and I’m careful about what I say yes to — because time is the real budget.

For quick glimpses of my process, work-in-progress clips, and visual notes, social channels are useful — but email remains the most reliable channel for real conversations.

Archive

The archive is a living pile: early experiments, rough cuts, outtakes, collages, sound tests, and pieces that exist only as notes. It is not curated to feel “finished”; rather, it is a place to return to, rework, and reimagine. Some pieces remain as fragments because they are useful fragments.

Over the years, I’ve learned to value process over prestige. Projects that were once abandoned often reappear as the seed for something new. The archive keeps those seeds — raw footage, audio scraps, and half-built edits — precisely because they remind me how the work is made.

If you’d like to see specific archive material, reach out; certain items are available for viewings or conversation. The archive is an invitation to see the artist as a maker of attempts, not only polished outputs.
